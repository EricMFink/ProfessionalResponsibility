Pro Bono Service

Key Concepts

•   Every lawyer has a professional responsibility to provide pro bono services.

•   A lawyer should provide a substantial majority of these pro bono hours without expectation of a fee to persons of limited means or to organizations that primarily address the needs of persons of limited means.

•   The responsibility to provide pro bono services is not subject to disciplinary process.

Introduction

You have already learned that a lawyer is a “public citizen having special responsibility for the quality of justice.”1 This special responsibility means that a lawyer should “devote professional time and resources … to ensure equal access to our system of justice for all those who because of economic or social barriers cannot afford or secure legal counsel.”2 The duties embedded in this responsibility respond to what often is termed the “access to justice crisis.”

The access to justice crisis is rooted in the financial inability of a substantial number of individuals to access the necessary resources to participate meaningfully and equally in our civil system of justice.3 Because of the legal knowledge and training 222often needed to navigate our civil justice system, these necessary resources typically include the assistance of a licensed legal practitioner.4 Yet, “[a]ccording to most estimates, about four-fifths of the civil legal needs of the poor … remain unmet.”5 The access to justice crisis, moreover, extends beyond poor and low-income individuals: “two- to three-fifths of the needs of middle-income individuals” also remain unfulfilled.6

The Rules of Professional Conduct look to lawyers to fill some of this access-to-justice gap.7 As a “public citizen,” lawyers already have a special responsibility for ensuring that our system of justice secures access to justice for individuals, regardless of financial means.8 Building on this public citizen responsibility, Rule 6.1 codifies a lawyer’s responsibility regularly to provide pro bono services—legal services to persons of limited means without expectation of a fee.

The Rule

Rule 6.1 begins by expressing a lawyer’s responsibility to provide pro bono services.

1.   Pro Bono Responsibility

Rule 6.1 establishes the responsibility of every lawyer to provide “voluntary” pro bono services:

223

Voluntary Pro Bono Publico Service

A lawyer should render public interest legal service. A lawyer may discharge this responsibility by providing professional services at no fee or a reduced fee to persons of limited means or to public service or charitable groups or organizations, by service in activities for improving the law, the legal system or the legal profession, and by financial support for organizations that provide legal services to persons of limited means.

The first question you should raise from Rule 6.1 is whether the Rule imposes an enforceable duty on lawyers to perform pro bono services. The language of the Rule suggests that the answer is yes.9 Further, mandatory pro bono would be consistent with the objective of the Rule to improve access to justice. Yet, the answer largely is no; although this Rule does establish a professional responsibility applicable to every lawyer, the comments make clear that enforcement of the Rule “is not intended to be enforced through disciplinary process.”10

The second question you should ask is how many hours of pro bono service should be performed a year. While the Rules of Professional Conduct do not specify how many hours of pro bono service are expected, other states do. For example, Washington mandates that “a lawyer should aspire to render at least (30) hours of pro bono publico legal services per year.”11 The A.B.A. Model Rules, by contrast, list fifty hours of pro bono service as the expected norm.12

The resulting dichotomy of an unenforceable professional responsibility reflects major compromises surrounding pro bono services as this Rule developed.13 Lawyers have objected to pro bono services because it uniquely burdens lawyers with forced charity, it generates concerns about competent representation, it is impractical to expect busy lawyers to perform this service every year, and it is problematic to monitor this activity effectively for discipline.14 Supporters of pro bono services, by contrast, have argued that a pro bono duty is appropriate because of lawyers’ unique monopoly over legal services, and it fills critical legal needs, offers public 224relations and business opportunities, attracts talented lawyers, and enhances lawyers’ emotional well-being within an often stressful profession.15

States largely have followed the Rules’ compromise and do not discipline lawyers for failing to provide pro bono services. Some states, however, have employed other measures to incentivize pro bono services. For example, New York State recently adopted a rule requiring that Bar applicants perform fifty hours of pro bono service as a condition to being admitted.16 Other states have adopted mandatory pro bono reporting requirements, where a lawyer can be disciplined for failing to report to the Bar on how much pro bono service he or she performed in that reporting cycle.17 States also publicly recognize lawyers who perform high levels of pro bono service18 and provide business incentives in exchange for pro bono service, such as referrals and free or reduced-cost continuing legal education.19

Despite these measures, pro bono service remains effectively voluntary, and thus, “ultimately rests upon the individual lawyer.”20 Lawyers, however, often do not practice only as individuals, but practice in a firm environment, where billable hours and top-down business pressures may limit the ability of individuals lawyers to fulfill this responsibility. Some versions of the Rules of Professional Conduct recognize this reality and note that “[l]aw firms should act reasonably to enable and encourage all lawyers in the firm to provide the pro bono legal services called 225for by this Rule.”21 Moreover, these Rules acknowledge that “at times it may be more feasible to satisfy the pro bono responsibility collectively, as by a firm’s aggregate pro bono activities.”22 Nevertheless, like lawyers, law firms are not subject to disciplinary process for failing in their pro bono responsibilities.23

One universal norm of pro bono service is that to constitute pro bono, legal work must be provided “without fee or at a substantially reduced fee.”24 This standard may mean that “the intent of the lawyer to render free legal services is essential.”25 As a result, lawyers cannot use pro bono as a backup to the deadbeat client: “services rendered cannot be considered pro bono if an anticipated fee is uncollected.”26

Consider whether this lawyer honored her pro bono responsibilities under Model Rule 6.1:

Example. Lawyer Agenda practiced at a large private law firm in the firm’s litigation department. Agenda was very experienced and billed several hundred dollars an hour for her services. Agenda, however, was interested in public law issues, and welcomed opportunities to represent parties she favored in civil rights litigation. Recently, Agenda won a major case in which she represented Angry Parents, a coalition of parents who opposed diversity policies adopted by the local school board. Agenda did not request or expect a fee when she agreed to represent Angry Parents. But, when Agenda won the case, she sought attorney’s fees, which this jurisdiction authorized. Agenda sought fees at her market rates, which over the life of the case totaled $1.8 million dollars. The school board claimed that this award would exceed its insurance policy coverage and drain its operating budget. Agenda responded, “Too bad. Don’t violate the Constitution if you don’t want to pay the cost of litigation against you.” At the same time, Agenda reported her time representing Angry Parents to her State Bar as pro bono service, and requested that she be listed in the Bar’s “Pro Bono Honor Roll” Bulletin. Agenda planned to market this Bulletin to current and prospective clients who express interest in public service. May Agenda properly claim that she performed pro bono service in representing Angry Parents?

226

Analysis. Agenda agreed to represent Angry Parents without fee or expectation of fee, so she began the case in position properly to claim pro bono service to her State Bar.27 Furthermore, the Rules of Professional Conduct provide that “the award of statutory attorney’s fees in a case originally accepted as pro bono would not disqualify such services from inclusion under this section.”28 Therefore, the fact alone that Agenda was paid attorney’s fee after winning the case would not prevent her from claiming this work as pro bono service to her State Bar.

Rule 6.1, however, further encourages lawyers in this circumstance “to contribute an appropriate portion of such fees to organizations or projects that benefit persons of limited means.”29 This comment does not require this action as a condition to claiming pro bono service, so Agenda still might be able to keep the attorney’s fees and claim pro bono service. Yet, the same comment does not approve of any attorney’s fees as consistent with pro bono service—just that attorney’s fees do not disqualify the lawyer from claiming pro bono service. Agenda billed her full market rate at several hundred dollars an hour. Recent high-profile cases have raised the question of whether lawyers who bill large firm market rates in claiming attorney’s fees still may claim that work as pro bono service, because these market rates so exceed any conception of pro bono service.30

Agenda accordingly should be cautious about claiming this work as pro bono service to her State Bar: if she claimed market rates for representing Angry Parents, Agenda arguably did not fulfill the spirit, if not the letter, of Model Rule 6.1. Agenda could avoid this dilemma by donating her profit to an appropriate cause, as some firms in this position have done.31 At a minimum, Agenda should be careful about marketing this work as pro bono to current or prospective clients who express interest in public service, as they could be misled by this claim if Agenda in fact pocketed market rates.32

2.   Pro Bono Activities

In addition to defining an unenforceable pro bono responsibility, Rule 6.1 identifies the types of activities that can satisfy this responsibility.

Voluntary Pro Bono Publico Service

A lawyer may discharge this responsibility by providing professional services at no fee or a reduced fee to persons of limited means or to public service or charitable groups or organizations, by service in activities for improving the law, the legal system or the legal profession, and by financial support for organizations that provide legal services to persons of limited means.

Rule 6.1 is detailed, but expresses a simple point: a lawyer has a lot of flexibility in how and for whom the lawyer can perform pro bono services.33 Ultimately, “providing legal services for those unable to pay” is prioritized.34 But a variety of factors may limit a lawyer’s ability to satisfy pro bono responsibility solely through this means. In that instance, the rest of the Rule indicates other ways for lawyers to satisfy their responsibility.35

Rule 6.1 therefore establishes an aspirational rule: lawyers have a professional responsibility to provide pro bono services, but are not subject to disciplinary process for failing in that responsibility. Nevertheless, many state bars and courts work hard to help lawyers find realistic and fulfilling ways to serve persons in need. As “public citizen,” you should take that responsibility seriously and look for and respond to these opportunities for public service.

228

Quick Summary

Rule 6.1 establishes the professional responsibility of every lawyer to provide pro bono services. A lawyer should aspire to satisfy this responsibility by providing direct legal services to persons of limited means or through other types of no- or reduced-fee services outlined in the rule.
